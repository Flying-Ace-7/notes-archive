## Ionic bond

An Ionic bond is formed when one or more valence electrons are donated from a metal ion to a non-metal ion to achieve octet/duplet/stable configuration.  Hence, the mettalic ion becomes positively charged, while the non-metal ion is negatively charged.

_This results in electrostatic  forces of attraction in between those particles_

For eg.

$$
\text{Cl}_{2} \text{(g)}+\text{2Na} \rightarrow \text{2NaCl}
$$
The strength of the ionic bond is defined as its lattice energy.

> [!note] Lattice energy
> Lattice energy is defined as the energy released to the surroundings by the formation of one mole of solid crystalline ionic compound formed from its gaseous ions.
> 
> Basically, the energy released when one mole of ionic compund froms. A stronger ionic bond will have a higher _**magnitude**_ of _lattice energy_.
> Formal definition:
> Lattice energy is the energy released when gaseous ions bind to form an ionic solid. Hence, this value will always be negative.

The higher the lattice energy, the higher the boiling and melting points of the substance.

### Factors affecting strength of Ionic bond

$$
E \propto \frac{q_{1}q_{2}}{r}
$$
Here, we see that the lattice energy is proportional to the magnitude of charges of the two ions over the internuclear distances of the ions/ionic radii .


### Ionic radius

When the radius of the ions increases, the ions are spaced further from each other, the distance between them increases, leading to a weaker forces of attrctions through electrostatic forces in between the ions.

For eg,
$$
\text{In ionic compunds, NaCl and KBr, which ionic compound has a higher melting point? (Q 1.1)}
$$
We observe that while sodium has the same ionic charge of +1 as K, it has a smaller ionic radius.
Similarly, Cl has the same ionic charge of -1 as Br, but a smaller ionic radius.
Hence, the internuclear distance inbetween ions in $NaCl$ is smaller than that of $KBr$, leading to stronger elctrostatic forces of attraction in between the ions in $NaCl$, and more energy is required to overcome them, hence it has a higher melting point.




### Magnitude Charge of ions


><mark class="hltr-red">The higher the charge on the cation and the anion, the stronger the electrostatic forces of attraction in between them</mark>



### Coloumbs Law
The correlation for lattice energy is referenced from Coloumbs law, which states that:

$$
\text{F} = k \frac{q_{1}q_{2}}{r^2}
$$
> [!tip] In summary, the larger the magnitude of charges on the ions/ the smaller the radius of the ions, the larger the magnitude of lattice energy of the compound, and hence have a higher melting/boiling point.

## Covalent Bond

A covalent bond when there is a sharing of electrons between non-metal atoms

Eg:
$Cl_2$
The strenght of the covalent bond is measured in terms of its bond energy ( or bond enthalpy), which is defined as the energy required to break an covalent bond, in one mole of an gaseous substance.
We say that the bond length is inversely proportional to bond energy, the shorter the bond length, the stronger the covalent bond.
Hence, the factors affecting the strength of a covalent bond is 
1. Atomic radius (The smaller the radius, the stronger the covalent bond)
2. Bond order (Single, double, triple bond)

For eg, the bond order of $Cl_2$ is 1, while the bond order of $\text{O}_2$ is 2, hence oxygen has the shorter bond length

The bond order is more significant than the atomic radius

## Metallic bond

Factors affecting strength of mettallic bond:

### Number of Electrons delocalised per metal atom in the structure
In a metal, the metal atoms are arranged ina regular fashion in rows, and their valence electrons are all delocalised, which form an 'sea' of electrons around the metal cations. hence, when there are more valence electrons delocalised, the electrostatic forces of attraction in between the sea of electrons and metal cations is greater, leading to a higher melting point.

### Size of metal cation
With smaller metal cations, they will be packed together more closely, leading stronger forces of attraction in between them, stronger metallic bond.

### Properties of metals

- High melting point
- Malleable (hammered into thin sheets/various shapes) and ductile (can be pulled into wires)
- Good conductor of electricity and thermal energy
Why are metals malleable?
Metal atoms are arranged in regular rows, and can slide over one another easily. The sea of electrons also help prevent repulsion inbetween the layers.


## Electronegativity and Polar bonds

> [!tip] Electronegativity is defined as the ability of an atom in a molecule to attract bonding electrons towards itself

\- basically, how well it can attract electrons to itself

The most 4 electronegative elements (Must know):
- **F**lourine
- **O**xygen
- **Cl**orine
- **N**itrogen

> [!note] Extension: Electron Affinity
> 
|                                             |                                                               |
| ------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Electronegativity                           | Electron affiinity                                            |
| The ability of an atom to attract electrons | Energy Chnage when an isolated gaseous atom gains an electron |
| Estimated value                             | Experimentally measurable quantity                            |


When an bond is formed, the difference in electronegativity in between the molecules can be used to determine the nature of their bonds.

The greater the difference, the more polar the bond is.
Why?
Difference in electronegativity results in unequal sharing of electrons in a bond, such that they are attracted to on atom more than the other, giving them a slight positive($\delta^+$) and negative charges ($\delta^-$). (The more electronegative atom has the $\delta^-$ charge).
When the difference in the electronegativity gets too large (eg, in between chlorine and sodium, non-metal and metal), the electons get fully transfered to the non-metal atom.

Usually, institutions set  values for the difference in electronegativity, for which, if the difference is greater than the value, it is an ionic bond.

On the other hand, the difference in electronegativity can be really small or 0, resulting in equal sharing of the electrons. This causes a non-polar bond, with no charges. There is also usually a limit for this, in which if the difference in electronegativity is below that limit, the bond is considered non-polar.

For CM3131, we take it that all bonds between atoms not from the same element are polar, with the exception being a $C-H$ bond. So, a $O=O$ bond is not polar, but a $F-O-F$ is.
